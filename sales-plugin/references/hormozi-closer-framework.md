# Alex Hormozi Sales Framework — Applied to {YOUR_CRM} Discovery Calls

> **Purpose**: Coaching philosophy and applied frameworks for {YOUR_CRM} discovery calls. The goal of every call is to close to the $3,000 audit — not to sell the full build.

---

## Core Philosophy

**Sell the vacation, not the plane.**

Don't sell CRM features. Don't sell the platform. Don't sell technology. Sell the dream outcome: a business where the team works on revenue instead of admin, where every answer is one search away, where your data actually tells you what to do next.

**The product is certainty.** Prospects are scared of wasting money on software that doesn't deliver. Every objection is a risk objection. Your job is to eliminate risk while amplifying the cost of inaction.

**Three things that kill discovery calls:**
1. Talking more than the prospect (should be 60/40 their favour)
2. Pitching before labelling the problem
3. No clear committed next step at the end

## The CLOSER Framework — Applied to {YOUR_CRM} Discovery Calls

### C — Clarify: Why Are You On This Call Today?

**Purpose**: Get them to articulate their own reason for being there. People buy their own reasons, not yours.

**Company-specific opener**:
- "Before I show you anything — what made you decide to get on this call?"
- "What's the thing in your operations that made you think 'I need to sort this out'?"
- "What's the specific problem you're hoping we can help with?"

**What to listen for**:
- Pen-and-paper operations (we've heard this: {CASE_STUDY_1} — 40 staff doing everything manually)
- Legacy systems (Microsoft Access via remote desktop, FileMaker Pro, Outlook-only)
- Growth-phase break point ("we're at 35 people and the systems aren't keeping up")
- AI curiosity ("we've heard about AI, the way it's going")
- Tool sprawl ("we're using 6 different things and they don't talk to each other")

**Red flag**: If they can't articulate a specific pain, they're browsing — not buying. Qualify harder before proceeding.

---

### L — Label: Name Their Problem Before They Do

**Purpose**: Show them you understand their situation better than they do. Label the pattern — it builds trust and makes the solution feel inevitable.

**The three labels to use**:

1. **SaaS Waste**: "So what I'm hearing is — you're paying for multiple systems that don't talk to each other, your team is spending time on bridging the gap between them, and you're probably duplicating data entry."

2. **Efficiency Drain**: "It sounds like your best people — the ones you hired to do the actual work — are spending a significant chunk of their week doing admin that could be automated. Is that right?"

3. **AI Blindness**: "And because your data is scattered across [X tools], you can't actually use AI to help you run the business — because there's no single source of truth to query. So you hear about AI but can't actually apply it."

**The confirming question after labelling**: "Does that feel like an accurate summary of where you're at?"

**When they say yes** — you've created alignment. Now you can present the solution without it feeling like a pitch.

**Do NOT label and immediately pitch.** Pause. Let them confirm. Then let them expand. The more they talk about their own pain, the more motivated they become.

---

### O — Overview: What Have They Already Tried?

**Purpose**: Understand what they've already attempted so you don't pitch solutions they've tried. Also surfaces how serious the pain is (if they've spent money trying to fix it, it's real pain).

**Company-specific questions**:
- "Have you tried any tools or systems before to solve this? What happened?"
- "Did you look at HubSpot or Salesforce? What put you off?"
- "Have you had a developer build something custom before?"
- "What's the main reason those previous attempts didn't stick?"

**Patterns we've seen in real calls**:
- FileMaker Pro (expensive, not web-based, VAs can't access it remotely)
- Microsoft Access with remote desktop (clunky, mobile unusable)
- Outlook only (no pipeline visibility, no automation, information trapped in inboxes)
- "Our system works okay but won't scale" — they know the problem, haven't committed to solving it

**Use this section to differentiate**: "A lot of what we hear from people who've tried [tool] is [specific limitation]. What we do differently is..."

### S — Sell the Vacation: Paint the Dream Outcome

**Purpose**: Before talking about features, get them to picture and own the desired future state. They need to say it, not just hear it.

**The dream for {YOUR_CRM} clients**:
> "Imagine it's Monday morning. Your team logs into one dashboard. Sales can see their pipeline, tasks, and client history. Your admin team isn't re-entering data from anyone. You type a question into the AI — 'which invoices are overdue?' or 'what projects are at risk this week?' — and you get an answer in 3 seconds. Your cash flow is visible in real time. Your best people are spending their time on the work that makes you money, not on admin."

**Discovery questions to co-create the dream**:
- "If you woke up tomorrow and the operational chaos was completely solved — what would your business look like?"
- "What would your team be doing with those recovered hours?"
- "If you could ask your business data any question and get an instant answer — what would be the first thing you'd ask?"
- "What would it mean for you personally if your team just... handled things, without you being the bottleneck?"

**Anchor to specific numbers**: Once they describe the dream, immediately anchor it in dollars:
- "So if your admin team saved even 5 hours a week each — at [their hourly rate], that's [$X/week] back into the business. Every week. Fifty-two weeks a year."
- "And that's just one team, one process. The efficiency gains compound across the whole business."

### E — Explain Concerns: The AAA Method for Objections

**Acknowledge / Associate / Ask** — never argue, never defend.

#### Objection: "I can't afford it" / "It's too expensive"
- **Acknowledge**: "Completely fair — this is a significant decision."
- **Associate**: "Most of our clients said the same thing before we looked at the numbers together. Scott [insert real client] was managing 30 sales staff manually, re-entering paper contracts into Excel every day. When we ran the math on what that was costing in admin time alone, the platform paid for itself in under 3 months."
- **Ask**: "Can we spend 2 minutes and actually look at what your current setup is costing you? I want to show you the math — not a slide deck, just your actual numbers."

#### Objection: "We don't have time for implementation"
- **Acknowledge**: "Time pressure is real — and you've already got a full plate."
- **Associate**: "The reason most of our clients say that is because they're picturing a long, painful IT project. What we actually do is start with a 2-4 week audit — which requires about 3-4 hours of your team's time total. We do the heavy lifting."
- **Ask**: "If the audit takes 2-4 weeks and costs your team maybe a morning of interviews — is that the kind of time commitment you could make?"

#### Objection: "We need to think about it"
- **Acknowledge**: "Of course — this is a meaningful decision."
- **Associate**: "Usually when I hear 'I need to think about it', it means there's a specific concern I haven't addressed yet. That's my fault, not yours."
- **Ask**: "What's the main thing that would need to be true for this to be a clear yes?"

#### Objection: "We already have [tool]"
- **Acknowledge**: "That makes sense — you've already invested in trying to solve this."
- **Associate**: "A lot of companies use [tool] for one piece and then still juggle [3 other tools] for everything else. The ROI isn't in any one tool — it's in eliminating the context switching between all of them."
- **Ask**: "How many other systems does your team use alongside [tool]? Because if it's more than two, that's where the efficiency is being lost."

#### Objection: "We're not sure the team will adopt it"
- **Acknowledge**: "Change fatigue is one of the most common blockers we hear."
- **Associate**: "The interesting thing is — we're not asking your team to learn a new tool. We're asking them to log into fewer tools. One dashboard instead of five. That's actually less effort, not more."
- **Ask**: "Which part of the change worries you most — training, resistance from specific people, or just the disruption during rollout?"

#### Objection: "Why not just use Zoho/SimPro/HubSpot?" (Chad's team, {PROSPECT_1})
- **Acknowledge**: "That's a really fair question — those tools exist for a reason."
- **Associate**: "Zoho/SimPro was built for the whole market, not your business. It does 70–80% of what you need, and you end up building workarounds for the rest. We've actually helped businesses migrate off those tools."
- **Ask**: "How much time is your team spending on the 20% it doesn't do? Because that's the real cost — not the licence fee."

#### Objection: "Will AI put my staff out of a job?" (Chad's accountant)
- **Acknowledge**: "That concern comes up a lot — and it's a real fear for anyone whose job involves admin work."
- **Associate**: "What we automate is the 80% that's repetitive and boring — re-entering data, chasing approvals, generating reports. Every business we've worked with has re-deployed their admin team into more revenue-driving activities, not reduced headcount."
- **Ask**: "Which roles in your team are spending the most time on that kind of repetitive admin work right now?"

#### Objection: "Will you still be around to support this in 5 years?" / "We're worried about being left stranded" (Chad)
- **Acknowledge**: "That's exactly the right thing to think about before committing to a custom build."
- **Associate**: "You own everything — code, data, infrastructure. If we disappeared tomorrow, any competent developer could pick up where we left off using the documentation we provide. You're not locked in. We also offer a full code handover package if you ever want to take things fully in-house."
- **Ask**: "Is the concern more about technical continuity — or about the relationship and ongoing support?"

### R — Reinforce: Lock In the Commitment to the Audit

**Purpose**: Confirm the next step is specific, time-bound, and mutual. Not "I'll think about it" — a committed date to kick off or a specific follow-up action.

**The reinforce sequence**:
1. Summarise what they told you: "So based on what you've shared — [X employees], [specific pain], losing roughly [Y hours/week] — you're leaving approximately [$Z/year] on the table."
2. Connect to the guarantee: "The audit starts at $3,000. If we don't find at least $5,000/month in savings — you pay nothing. We take all the risk."
3. Offer the specific next step: "The next step is simple: I'll send you an email today with an invoice link. Pay it and we kick off immediately — I'll send you the onboarding questionnaire the same day."
4. Create light urgency: "We only take on a handful of audits each month to maintain quality — I want to make sure we can hold a spot for you."

**If they're not ready to pay today**:
- "Totally fine. What specific date does it make sense to revisit this?"
- "What would need to happen between now and [date] for this to be the right time?"
- "Is there one person in your organisation whose buy-in would make this a definite yes?"

## The Value Equation — Applied to {YOUR_CRM}

From Hormozi: Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Success) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)

**Maximise the numerator**:
- **Dream Outcome**: Business runs without manual chaos, team works on revenue, AI-powered visibility into everything, competitors falling behind
- **Perceived Likelihood**: 90-day ROI guarantee, $5K/month savings guarantee on audit, track record (300+ projects, {YOUR_CREDENTIAL} on Upwork), client testimonial ({TESTIMONIAL_NAME}: "well under budget, within timeframes")

**Minimise the denominator**:
- **Time Delay**: "We can kick off within 1–2 weeks. ROI visible by week 12."
- **Effort & Sacrifice**: "We do the heavy lifting. The audit requires ~3-4 hours of your team's time total. For the build — we run the project, you show up for sessions."

**The framing**: "You're not buying a software project. You're buying operational certainty. In 2-4 weeks, you know exactly where you're losing money and exactly what to do about it."

## Grand Slam Offer — How the Audit Hits All 9 Components

The $3,000 audit is structured as a Grand Slam Offer:

1. **Dream Outcome** — "Complete clarity on where your business is losing money, exactly how much, and a concrete plan to fix it"
2. **Perceived Likelihood** — Money-back guarantee + track record + everything you own regardless
3. **Time Delay** — 2-4 weeks start to answer (not 16 weeks)
4. **Effort** — "We do the heavy lifting — 3-4 hours of your team's time"
5. **Price Anchoring** — "$15,000 worth of deliverables, your investment $3,000"
6. **Risk Reversal** — 100% money-back if we don't find $5K/month in savings
7. **Scarcity** — "We only take on a handful of audits each month"
8. **Bonus** — "One FREE automation implemented in week 1"
9. **Credit** — "100% credited toward the build — audit is effectively free if you proceed"

## Talk Ratio Targets

**Target**: 60% prospect talking / 40% salesperson

**Why this matters**: Gong research shows top salespeople talk less than 43% of the time on discovery calls. The more the prospect talks, the more they sell themselves.

**Tier 1 triggers** — if you notice any of these in yourself, it's time to ask a question:
- Monologue longer than 90 seconds (ask an open-ended question)
- You've talked for 3+ turns in a row without a prospect response
- You're explaining features the prospect hasn't asked about
- You're using filler ("basically", "essentially", "kind of", "like")

**Talk ratio reset questions**:
- "What's your reaction to that?"
- "Does that resonate with what you're seeing?"
- "What would that mean for your business?"
- "Have you seen that problem in your team?"

## Buying Signals to Watch For

**High-intent signals** (prospect is close to committing):
- "That's exactly our problem" — after you label their situation
- "When could we start?" — forward-looking language
- "What does the process look like?" — asking about next steps
- "How long does it take?" — thinking about timeline
- Questions about specific features relevant to their business
- "Can you send me something to share with [partner/boss]?" — involves someone else
- Emotional language: "we're drowning", "it's a nightmare", "drives me crazy"

**Low-intent signals** (don't push, requalify):
- "We're just looking around"
- "We're not in a rush"
- "Send me a proposal and I'll think about it" (without a follow-up call scheduled)
- Short, closed answers throughout the call (not engaged)

**From real calls — specific moments that signalled genuine interest**:
- {PROSPECT_1}: "We've heard about AI, the way it's going" — expressed genuine curiosity about AI even with no current systems
- {PROSPECT_1}: "We do everything by pen and paper" + $18M revenue — massive ROI opportunity with zero tech complexity to migrate from
- {PROSPECT_4}: "We're in a growth phase" — implied timing pressure
- {PROSPECT_5}: "I want data entry to be through voice on iPad" — knows exactly what he wants, just needs confidence to proceed
- {PROSPECT_3}: "I'm trying to get in early before it gets complicated" — proactive buyers are easier closes; they don't need convincing the problem exists
- Any prospect who describes their current system as something they "built themselves" and are "outgrowing" — they know it needs replacing, they just need permission

## When to Close to the Audit

**The right moment** is when:
1. They've described their own pain in specific terms (not your words, theirs)
2. They've agreed the status quo is costing them money
3. They understand the demo enough to believe it could work
4. No unresolved risk concerns remain

**The close sequence** (use this verbatim or close to it):
1. "Based on what you've told me — [restate their pain in their words] — you're looking at a real cost of [estimate their efficiency loss]."
2. "The next step isn't to jump straight into a build. What makes sense is to start with our operational audit."
3. "It takes 2-4 weeks. We interview your team, map your processes, quantify the waste, and give you a detailed plan — plus one automation implemented in week 1."
4. "It's $3,000. That gets credited 100% toward the build if you proceed. And if we don't find at least $5,000/month in savings — you pay nothing."
5. "I'll send you an email today with the invoice. Pay it and we kick off immediately."

**If they hesitate**: "What's the one thing that would need to be different for this to be a clear yes?"

**If they ask for a full proposal first**: "I'd actually be doing you a disservice if I gave you a full build proposal without the audit — we'd be guessing at your numbers. The audit is how we give you an accurate proposal. That's the whole point."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid (From Real Call Analysis)

Based on AI analysis of actual discovery calls:

1. **Too much talking about features, not enough listening** — Let them describe the problem. Don't jump to "our platform does..."
2. **Closed questions** — Don't ask "Do you use a CRM?" Ask "Walk me through how you manage your sales pipeline today."
3. **Skipping the label** — Don't pitch before you've confirmed they feel understood. "So what I'm hearing is..." before any solution talk.
4. **Vague close** — "I'll send you some info" is not a next step. "I'll send the invoice today and we can kick off Monday" is a next step.
5. **Talking about the build on a discovery call** — The goal is to sell the audit. Don't price the build, don't scope the build, don't timeline the build. Sell the audit.
6. **Explaining the guarantee too late** — Mention the 90-day ROI guarantee and the $5K/month audit guarantee early. It eliminates risk before it becomes an objection.
7. **Not quantifying in their specific numbers** — Don't say "$390K/year for a 10-person team." Say "With your 35 staff, at roughly $50/hr blended, saving 15 hours per person per week is $1.4M/year in recovered productivity."
